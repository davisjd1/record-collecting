Most important things to remember

Why am I collecting?
You are in this for the love of the music, not to make money. It is exciting to find a great sounding album at crazy good prices.
Why can't I make money doing this if I'm getting things so cheaply?
You don't have time to resell records. If you are going to sell the entire collection some day, that's fine, but you'll need to send it as a single lot. It takes too much time and effort to list albums individually on CL or Ebay.
Don't spend more than $10 on an album, no matter how much you want it.
If it costs more than that, it's not worth it. Record store dealers list records at crazy prices for things that aren't in perfect shape, all because they think that record collectors are collecting records for resale eventually, and not for music enjoyment now.
What should I be spending on classical albums?
You got back into record collecting in particular because you inherited Dad's collection. Then you started finding clasical albums you didn't already have on LP at $.50 and $1.00 (actually 25% off that). The most you've spent on one LP so far is $5. Don't spend much more than that for a single album. If the prices are higher than that check Tidal, Freegal, Spotify, Hoopla, YouTube - in that order.
But I think I can make money on this album
If you are thinking that, then it's because you already have it on LP, SACD, or CD. Stop it. The only time you will ever make money selling records is if you fall into a super amazing deal you can't pass up and you already have a buyer for the entire lot. Consider how much moeny and effort it will take in boxing, moving, storing, and selling. Again, you will not sell records individually.
But I really think I can make money on this album
Garage sale, maybe, and only if you bought them at less than $.25 each. There is no way you will sell all the albums at a weekend garage sale. You might not sell any. You have already sold items before and didn't make money on them. Remember those times.

RCA Living Stereo!

The Living Stereo shaded dogs in the red label sound the best. These are also the most expensive, and can fetch prices upwards of a couple hundred dollars. They're way out of your price-range. But, you might get lucky like you have before. You own all the RCA Living Stereo SACDs that Sony released in the 2000s. You also own a few that Analogue Productions have released the last few years. You have several shaded dog mono LPs that sound great and were released simultaneously with the first run of Living Stereo albums. The white dog on black background came after. They also sound great. You have several. The white dog on red (no shading) came after and are reissues. They sound ok. After this, RCA Living Stereo were released with Dynagroove technology, which was basically high compression (less dynamic range). These sound better on crappy systems, but sound really bad on your system - WARNING - only buy these if the album was only released in Dynagroove, like your Death of the Bishop album.

Mercury Living Presence!

The Mercury Living Presence albums come in mono and stereo (as opposed to RCA's Living Stereo which, duh). The monos sound fantastic as do the stereos. You don't own any stereos on LP, but you own the three CD box sets which are fantastic. If you can pick up any Living Presence Stereos for cheap, go for it. There were 20 of these released on SACD and the sound quality is amazing. Unfortunately, they're between $20 and $50. But, if you can find them for less, definitely pick them up because they sound better than the CDs you have.

Deutsche Grammophon Gesellschaft!

Deutsche Grammophon has excellent quality recordings throughout their run into the early '80s when everyone started recording digitally. The best sounding you've found so far have the round tulip border, Stereo written in the banner, centered below the entire writing of DGG. The later good recordings don't have Gesellschaft on it at all. Early records = mono is LPM and stereo is SLPM. You have several mono DGG and only a few early stereo DGG. Try for early stereo, but you can't go wrong - great performances and sound quality into digital era. You have Karajan conducting Beethoven's 9 Symphonies on Blu Ray audio. You also have Karajan's complete Richard Strauss box set on CD and Blu Ray audio. It doesn't get better than these, so no need to buy vinyl of these recordings.

Columbia Masterworks!

Columbia Masterworks are hidden treasures. These are easy to come by for cheap, they sound amazing, and have great performances and great conductors - early Bernstein and Eugene Ormandy in particular. You're only looking for two things, the first being the Columbia Six Eyes, which have the six microphones - three on left and three on right of spindle hole. Stereo Fidelity written at top. You have a few monos - they don't sound half as good, but are still decent. Slightly older stereo Six Eyes have CBS in the banner between Stereo and Fidelity, but you don't have any - yet. After that came the Two Eyes, which you have a few. Six eyes and two eyes both sound great. After that, sound quality decreases. Once they went to the brownish lable with orange lettering around the outside, the quality went downhill. You've had a few and sold all of them.

Everest Records

From 1958 to 1962ish Everest made some of the best recordings. Copland, Stokowski, etc. conducting amazing works. The mono recordings are super good as well, but get stereo where you can. There have been some reissues and re-releases, but not from Everest Records. Analogue Productions just released a few, and they're $40 a piece. Apparently they sound better than the originals, but the originals you have are fantastic, and you got them at great prices. You don't have any on CD or SACD, but there are some out there - if you see any, they have been reviewed very well. Many of the titles are available on Tidal, so when you need your Everest fix, you can go there.

Decca/London!

Everyone goes for the early Decca wideband labels and then narrowband. Decca had some of the best recording gear in the business and it shows. BUT, the wide vs. narrow band labels is just for sellers to make money. London was Decca's U.S. subsidiary, and used the exact same presses to make the records. As long as you get London records that were made in England, you're golden. In fact, the London's into the '70s with FFRR (full frequency range recordings) are fantastic, and again, can be found easily and cheaply because everyone is grabbing up the widebands that don't even sound as good. You don't have any Deccas - good luck! But, you have some London's on vinyl and love them.
